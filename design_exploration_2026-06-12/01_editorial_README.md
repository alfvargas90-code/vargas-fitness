# 01 · Editorial / Magazine — "The Daily Charge"

**One-line POV:** Your day's recovery as a morning newspaper — one headline metric, set in serif type on cream paper, that tells you how to live today instead of just what your numbers are.

## The point of view
This is the deliberate opposite of a dashboard. Where the current app shows *everything at once* on a dark grid, the editorial direction shows *one thing that matters* and frames it as narrative. Today the headline is the **−4.2 ANS Charge** — set like a magazine cover number, under a masthead ("The Daily Charge"), with a one-sentence editorial verdict: *"Your body is asking for a lighter day."*

Design language borrowed from print:
- **Serif display type**, drop cap, small-caps section labels, hairline rules, a dateline.
- **Cream/ink palette** with a single oxblood accent — calm, low-stimulation, the inverse of the dark gradient.
- **Single-column scroll** with generous whitespace; secondary metrics (HRV, resting HR, recharge, weight) demoted to a tidy "below the fold" spread.
- A hairline 7-day weight sparkline as a typographic flourish, not a chart you study.

## What user it serves best
The user who opens the app **once in the morning, reads it like a coffee ritual, and acts.** It is anti-anxiety by design: a negative recovery day becomes guidance ("honor it, let ambition wait"), not a red alarm. It serves Alfredo on the days he's overanalyzing — it gives a verdict and gets out of the way.

## Trade-offs
- Lowest data density of the four. Power users wanting to drill into every number will feel starved.
- The "voice" has to be written well every day or it reads as a fortune cookie — implies a copy/prose generation layer (which the existing Currents prose engine could feed).
